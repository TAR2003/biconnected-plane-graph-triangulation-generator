\section{Introduction}

The algorithm follows a tree-like structure for generating all valid planar triangulations of a biconnected planar graph. To prove the correctness and efficiency of our algorithm, we must sequentially prove different elements: completeness, existence of valid root triangulations, and complexity analysis.

\section{Proof of Completeness}

\subsection{Pruning Strategy}

Our algorithm employs pruning operations for triangulations where a blocking chord is a duplicate chord (i.e., the chord has occurred in earlier faces). When we encounter such duplication, we prune that triangulation and its entire branch.

\begin{theorem}[Completeness via Pruning]
All children of a pruned branch would have been invalid triangulations.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We classify triangulations as invalid when any one of their blocking chords is a duplicate chord. To prove completeness, we must demonstrate that a blocking chord is never flipped in the children of a branch.

Let chord $a$-$b$ be a blocking chord in any triangulation tree node. Since the node represents a triangulation, every face is triangulated. Therefore, there exist two other points that form triangles with the $a$-$b$ chord on both sides:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Let $c$ be the point on the opposite side of the root vertex
    \item Let $r$ be the point on the root side
\end{itemize}

We analyze three cases based on the position of $r$:

\textbf{Case 1: $r$ is on the left of the root vertex}

Flipping the $a$-$b$ chord would result in an $r$-$c$ chord. However, before flipping, the $b$-$r$ chord was also a blocking chord positioned to the left of $a$-$b$. This means $a$-$b$ could not be the leftmost blocking chord. Consequently, the new $r$-$c$ chord cannot be the leftmost blocking chord either, because the $r$-$b$ chord still exists and remains to the left of the $c$-$r$ chord. Therefore, flipping the $a$-$b$ blocking chord is not permitted by the algorithm in this case.

\textbf{Case 2: $r$ is on the right of the root vertex}

Flipping the $a$-$b$ chord would produce an $r$-$c$ chord. However, the $a$-$r$ chord is more to the left than the $r$-$c$ chord, making this flip invalid according to the algorithm.

\textbf{Case 3: $r$ is at the root}

Flipping the $a$-$b$ chord would create a root-$c$ chord. This would give the child triangulation's root vertex better visibility than the parent, which violates the algorithm's constraints.

In all three cases, flipping a blocking chord in a triangulation tree is impossible. Therefore, a blocking chord will be present in all of its children.

Thus, if a blocking chord is a duplicate chord, all child triangulations will contain this chord, making all child triangulations invalid. By pruning them, we do not ignore any valid triangulations.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Finding Valid Root Triangulations}

Having proved that our pruning method preserves all valid triangulations, we now address whether we can always find a root triangulation where no chord is duplicate or blocking.

\section{Existence of Valid Root Triangulation}

\begin{definition}[Root Triangulation]
A root triangulation is the root of the tree of triangulations, constructed by selecting a root vertex and adding chords from this root vertex to all non-neighboring vertices of the current face.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Safe Root Vertex]
A safe root vertex is one from which adding chords to every other non-neighboring node will not create a multi-edge problem. Formally, we must find a vertex such that none of the previous faces share a chord where one endpoint is the root vertex and the other endpoint is a vertex of the current face.
\end{definition}

\begin{theorem}[Existence of Safe Root]
In any face of a biconnected planar graph, there always exist at least two safe vertices that can serve as root vertices for valid triangulations.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Consider a face with $n$ vertices, numbered from 1 to $n$. Since we are working with a planar graph, chords can only exist in the outer plane.

\textbf{Case 1: No chords from previous faces}

If there are no chords from previous faces with both endpoints inside the current face, then all $n$ vertices are eligible to be safe vertices.

\textbf{Case 2: At least one chord from previous faces}

Suppose there exists a chord $a$-$b$ on the outer plane belonging to a previous face. Both $a$ and $b$ are disqualified from being safe vertices. However, this introduces an important constraint.

Due to planarity, chords cannot cross each other. Since the $a$-$b$ chord exists, no chord on the outer plane can overtake it. Consequently:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Nodes $(a+1, a+2, \ldots)$ might have connections between them (if not neighbors)
    \item They cannot have chords with nodes $(a-1, \ldots, b+1)$ as this would violate planarity by crossing the $a$-$b$ chord
\end{itemize}

This creates a specific structure we denote as $T_n$, where $n$ is the number of internal vertices between the endpoints of a constraining chord.

\subsection{Analysis of $T_n$ Structures}

The current face contains two complementary $T_n$ structures:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Structure 1: $a, a+1, \ldots, b$
    \item Structure 2: $a, a-1, \ldots, b$
\end{enumerate}

The internal vertices are numbered $1, \ldots, n-3$. The minimum structure is $T_1$ (since $T_0$ would mean $a$ and $b$ are neighbors, and neighbors cannot form chords). The maximum is $T_{n-3}$ (accounting for endpoints and the complementary structure).

\textbf{Base Case: $T_1$}

For $T_1$, there is one internal vertex $c$ between $a$ and $b$. Both $a$ and $b$ are neighbors of $c$, making any chord from $c$ impossible. Therefore, $c$ is a safe vertex.

\textbf{Base Case: $T_2$}

For $T_2$, with vertices $c$ and $d$ between $a$ and $b$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $c$ has a chord, it must be with $b$ (the only non-neighbor)
    \item This creates a $T_1$ structure containing $d$, making $d$ safe
    \item Similarly, if $d$ has a chord, it must be with $a$
    \item This creates a $T_1$ structure containing $c$, making $c$ safe
\end{itemize}

\textbf{General Case: $T_n$ for arbitrary $n$}

We present an algorithm to find a safe vertex in $O(n)$ time:

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Finding a Safe Vertex in $T_n$}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Consider vertex $a+1$
\State $a+1$ cannot create chords with neighbors $a$ and $a+2$
\State Possible chord partners: $\{a+3, \ldots, b\}$
\State Check from $b$ downward using a hashset (O(1) per check)
\If{chord $(a+1, b)$ exists}
    \State Structure becomes $T_{n-1}$ with vertices $a+2, \ldots, b-1$
\ElsIf{chord $(a+1, b-k)$ exists for some $k > 0$}
    \State Structure becomes $T_{n-k-1}$
\Else
    \State $a+1$ is a safe vertex (no chords with any candidate)
\EndIf
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

In each iteration:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If a chord is found, $n$ decreases, eventually reaching $T_1$ or $T_2$
    \item If no chord is found after checking all candidates, the vertex is safe
\end{itemize}

Since every check reduces the problem size or finds a safe vertex, and we've proven $T_1$ and $T_2$ contain safe vertices, every $T_n$ structure contains at least one safe vertex.

\textbf{Conclusion:}

From a face with no multi-edges in previous faces, all $n$ vertices can be safe. For faces with at least one multi-edge from previous faces, there are at least two complementary $T_n$ structures, each containing at least one safe vertex.

Therefore, every face in a biconnected planar graph, regardless of calculation stage, has at least two safe vertices. This guarantees the existence of at least two safe root triangulations regardless of the calculation order.
\end{proof}

\section{Completeness Summary}

We have now established:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The pruning method preserves all valid triangulations
    \item Safe root triangulations always exist
\end{enumerate}

Therefore, our algorithm covers all valid planar triangulations of any planar biconnected graph.

\section{Time Complexity Analysis}

\begin{theorem}[Amortized Linear Time Complexity]
The algorithm has amortized $O(1)$ time complexity per triangulation, or amortized linear time complexity overall.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Building Time}

\begin{definition}[Building Time]
The building time is the time required to calculate the safe root and construct the chords from the root vertex for a single face.
\end{definition}

From our algorithm and proofs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Finding a safe vertex: $O(n)$
    \item Adding all chords from the root: $O(n)$
    \item Total building time for a face with $n$ vertices: $O(n)$
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Minimum Number of Triangulations}

\begin{lemma}[Lower Bound on Triangulation Count]
For a face with $n$ vertices, there are at least $O(n)$ valid triangulations.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
We have proven that every face has at least two safe vertices. Let vertices $a$ and $c$ be two such safe vertices.

By our algorithm, starting with the $a$-root triangulation, we can reach the $c$-root triangulation through a series of chord flips. At each step, we flip exactly one chord, and each intermediate triangulation is valid (otherwise it would have been pruned).

Comparing $a$-root and $c$-root triangulations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $a$-root: all chords have endpoint $a$
    \item $c$-root: all chords have endpoint $c$
    \item Only one chord may be shared: $a$-$c$
    \item All other $n-4$ chords must be different
\end{itemize}

Therefore, transforming from $a$-root to $c$-root requires $n-4$ flips, each producing a valid triangulation.

Including the root triangulation: at least $n - 4 + 1 = n - 3$ triangulations exist.

Thus, there are at least $O(n)$ triangulations for a face with $n$ vertices.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Amortized Analysis}

Consider the recursive tree structure of our algorithm for a biconnected graph with multiple faces.

\textbf{Two-Face Graph:}

Let $n_1$ and $n_2$ be the number of vertices in faces 1 and 2, respectively.

\begin{align*}
\text{Total building time: } B &= n_1 + n_1 \cdot n_2 \\
\text{Total triangulations: } T &= n_1 \cdot n_2
\end{align*}

Since $n_2 \geq 3$:
\begin{align*}
n_1 \cdot 1 &< n_1 \cdot n_2 \\
B = n_1 + n_1 \cdot n_2 &< n_1 \cdot n_2 + n_1 \cdot n_2 \\
&= 2 \cdot n_1 \cdot n_2 \\
&= 2T
\end{align*}

Total time complexity: $< 3T$

\textbf{Three-Face Graph:}

Let $n_1$, $n_2$, $n_3$ be the number of vertices in the three faces.

\begin{align*}
B &= n_1 + n_1 \cdot n_2 + n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3 \\
T &= n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3
\end{align*}

From the two-face case: $n_1 + n_1 \cdot n_2 < 2 \cdot n_1 \cdot n_2$

Therefore:
\begin{align*}
B &< 2 \cdot n_1 \cdot n_2 + n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3
\end{align*}

Since $n_3 \geq 3$, we have $2 < n_3$:
\begin{align*}
2 \cdot n_1 \cdot n_2 &< n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3 \\
B &< n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3 + n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdot n_3 \\
&= 2T
\end{align*}

Total time complexity: $< 3T$

\textbf{General Case: $k$ Faces}

By induction, for any number of faces where each has at least 3 vertices:

\begin{align*}
B &= n_1 + n_1 \cdot n_2 + \cdots + n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_k \\
T &= n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_k
\end{align*}

The pattern holds:
\begin{align*}
n_1 + n_1 \cdot n_2 + \cdots + n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_{k-1} &< 2 \cdot n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_{k-1}
\end{align*}

Since $n_k \geq 3$:
\begin{align*}
2 \cdot n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_{k-1} &< n_1 \cdot n_2 \cdots n_k \\
B &< 2T
\end{align*}

Total time complexity: $< 3T$

\begin{corollary}
For any biconnected planar graph, the algorithm takes total time $< 3 \times (\text{number of triangulations})$. Therefore, the algorithm has amortized linear time complexity.
\end{corollary}

\section{Space Complexity Analysis}

\begin{theorem}[Linear Space Complexity]
The algorithm has $O(n)$ space complexity, where $n$ is the total number of vertices.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
When processing face $i$, we must store information for all faces $1, \ldots, i$.

The storage includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Vertex information: $n_1 + n_2 + \cdots + n_i$ vertices
    \item Chord information: For a planar graph, there are $n-3$ chords total
    \item A hashset for current chord lookup
\end{itemize}

Since the total number of vertices across all faces is $O(n)$ and the number of chords in a planar graph is also $O(n)$, the total space complexity is $O(n)$.

The hashset for storing current chord information also requires $O(n)$ space.

Therefore, the entire algorithm uses $O(n)$ space complexity.
\end{proof}

\section{Conclusion}

We have rigorously proven that our planar triangulation algorithm:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Is complete:} It finds all valid planar triangulations through proper pruning of invalid branches
    \item \textbf{Always has a starting point:} At least two safe root vertices exist in every face
    \item \textbf{Is efficient in time:} Amortized $O(1)$ time complexity per triangulation, or amortized linear overall
    \item \textbf{Is efficient in space:} $O(n)$ space complexity
\end{enumerate}

These properties make the algorithm both theoretically sound and practically efficient for enumerating all planar triangulations of biconnected planar graphs.
